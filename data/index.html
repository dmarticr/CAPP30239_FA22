Is Nintendo Falling Behind?

Nintendo is a Japanese video game company founded in 1889 (yes, more than a century ago!), known for creating some of the most popular video games in history, such as Mario, The Legend of Zelda, and Pok√©mon. Maybe you know that it distributed the 1st video console in Japan in 1997, but did you know it started off as a playing card company? (Not always for the whole family)

Digging into the company's history, it seems that Nintendo has been a leader in the video game industry for decades. In fact, Nintendo invigorated this market after its 80s crisis. However, the company has faced new challenges in recent times that have pushed it to reinvent itself, and some believe the company has fallen behind by releasing weaker consoles than its competitors.

But success is not measured by console power, and we are going to see why Nintendo is still a reference for the industry.

Console vs. Games

There are currently 3 manufacturers of video game consoles on the market: Microsoft, Nintendo, and Sony, what we will call the platforms. On the other hand, there is a large number of video game developers (publishers), including the console manufacturers (which normally make video games for their own consoles), as well as other companies that make video games, in general, for several consoles simultaneously (e.g., Activision and Electronic Arts).

Therefore, you can account for sales by platforms (all the videogames sold in a platform, from many developers), or sales by the developer (which makes videogames to one to multiple platforms). Nintendo plays on both sides.

From a platform perspective, Nintendo is not behind its competitors. The right graph shows that, based on the release date of the video games, Nintendo's new releases have outperformed those of Sony or Microsoft, whose profits leverage heavily on previously released games.

This is because, for a couple of years now, Nintendo has been trying to do something different with their consoles (while the rest of the market orbits around two consoles that do basically the same thing). This strategy has been working out phenomenally for Nintendo, focusing on portability and lower prices.

The latter is evident in the pie chart below which also dissects through the consoles they have made. Although the comparison is unfair (Nintendo is older), the variety of consoles that Nintendo have offered is greater (and cheaper) than their competitors. This has kept Nintendo in the leadership board, with historical sales exceeding those of its competitors. In this matter, the second position goes to Sony.

Favorable results are also observed when, instead of analyzing video game sales from a console hardware viewpoint, they are broken down according to software production (please press the Publisher button below).

Nintendo is the most profitable publisher followed by Activision and Electronic Arts (which make games for Nintendo consoles too), and notice that most of the video games from Sony and Microsoft platforms are not developed by them. Although this market is less concentrated than the platforms one (the "Others" block contains a huge number of developers smaller than the smallest identified developer on the left side of the pie: Take-Two Interactive), Nintendo still dominates.

Which video games do they do?

Nintendo games are concentrated into 2 main categories: platform games and role-playing games. Of the first, Nintendo accounts for around half of the historical sales of the entire market, while of the second category around a third (left chart).

Here is an interesting fact: platform games are nothing more than those games where you have to move a character from one place to another on the board (like Mario), and this category was practically born with a video game from this company in 1981: Donkey Kong, " the first to have a substantial narrative, along with a sense of humor".

Mario debuted in that video game as Jumpman, being one of the first characters to ever actually jump up in a video game (now it seems insubstantial, but that was such a hit at that time!).

Regarding latitudes, the database does not allow us to reach interesting conclusions, most video games are unclassified in this regard. For the classified, total market sales are concentrated in North America and Europe, while (unsurprisingly) Japanese Nintendo Sales exceed those of other regions.

Last but not least, Nintendo leads the top of most popular games of all time. Process Section

Why are you interested in the topic?

In general, the video game industry is essential not only for its size but because of its cultural influence, and Nintendo is a wide-known reference in popular culture. Hence, I found it attractive to any potential reader. Also, this database was rich enough to allow exploring data across many dimensions (e.g., cross-sectional and time series) making it ideal for data visualization techniques implementation.

What were other options for data and why did you choose this data set?

Before I got to this topic, I explored 3 databases:

1) My first option was violence in Colombia over time (my idea was to find some kind of change in the patterns of these figures as a result of the peace process); however, the database had many missing values and some elements of the analysis could be a sensitive topic for some people.

2) My second choice was child violence (derived from the violence databases explored in 1), but I did not find this topic appropriate/appealing either. Also, data quality was unsatisfactory.

3) Lastly, I found records of an animal shelter that tracked all the animals that arrived there. The database included the reason the animals came to the shelter (abandonment, return, etc.), their health condition, whether they found a family, etc. I discarded this dataset because it was difficult to distinguish multiple records of the same pet and, therefore, remove duplicates (also because I did not like the topic).

Where does the data come from? Include link to their website and where the data was collected.

This dataset was collected from www.vgchartz.com through web scrapping using BeautifulSoup on Python (code provided in the repository). The code for scraping is based on GregorUT/vgchartzScrape.

Unfortunately, www.vgchartz.com was down by the time of this deliverable, but you can check in Wikipedia that the "VGChartz Network is a collection of five video game websites: VGChartz, gamrFeed, gamrReview, gamrTV, and gamrConnect. VGChartz sits at the center of the network and is a video game sales tracking website, providing weekly sales figures of console software and hardware by region".

How did you define your story? Any pivots that you made.

Generally speaking, it started with an overall comparison between different game platforms and developers, then moved on to the games Nintendo has excelled at, and concluded with the company's place among the most popular games of all time. My story was linear. it began with a question whose answer could initially point in one direction, and I indicate the opposite to then chain evidence along the article to support my viewpoint while giving the reader additional information about Nintendo by way of fun facts.

Is any data missing? Do you have concerns about data gaps?

Of the 20,000 videogames included, only 2.5% (497) did not have a publication date, however, this did not affect the quality of the analysis due to its small proportion over the whole sample. In contrast, a significant number of observations (4,067 which represented 20.3% of the sample) were not classified by region, so any relevant insight could be delivered in this direction (mentioned in the article).

Any ethical consideration in the story telling or disclosures you would like to make about choices you made while visualizing?

None, the data is public and it did not contain any sensitive content.

Why did you choose the charts you chose? How do they help tell the story?

Although the choice of charts was made based on the information I wanted to show (and highlight) for each case, my decision incorporated the following criteria also:

1) I find using graphics with which the user can interact extremely attractive, so I was biased toward this direction.

2) I chose graphics that would allow me to go beyond what I learned in class, which would challenge me to learn new techniques.

3) Graphics that condensed various dimensions of the database, allowing the reader to explore the data on their own, without overloading him/her.

Any coding challenges or takeaways? Etc.

In general, the whole process was very enriching. My main difficulty was not having previous experience with HTML or JavaScript, and learning the basics on the fly while also understanding the logic of D3 was somewhat overwhelming. In fact, for example, in the third graphic (which has 4 buttons that update it), I copied and pasted data from 4 JSON files because I couldn't declare a JavaScript constant attached to each of these files (which conceptually doesn't seem to be complicated).

Another important lesson is maintaining a holistic view of the process, ensuring that code hierarchies are preserved while working on several .js files (learned the hard way).

Some elements and transitions that I wanted to implement were not part of this delivery due to the deadlines (e.g., I did not achieve the tooltip for the last chart appearing under the mouse every time the user hovers over each circle, and instead left it fixed to the right of the chart). However, the door was left open to continue exploring.

The main takeaway, from the class in general, was that this is an amazing topic and there is a lot to be learned.
